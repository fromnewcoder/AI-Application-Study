The fine-tuning mental model
Fine-tuning adapts a pre-trained model's weights on new data, teaching it style, domain knowledge, or behavior that prompting alone can't reliably achieve. Think of it as the difference between giving an employee a handbook (prompting) vs. a 3-month apprenticeship (fine-tuning).

The three-way choice: Prompt → RAG → Fine-tune
Prompt engineeringtry first
Best for: general tasks, few-shot examples, fast iteration. No training cost. Fails when behavior needs to be deeply consistent or model lacks domain knowledge.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)add knowledge
Best for: injecting up-to-date or private knowledge. Model still needs to reason well over retrieved chunks. Fails when the task is about style or behavior, not facts.
Fine-tuningchange the model
Best for: reliably reshaping model behavior, teaching new output formats, domain adaptation at scale. Highest upfront cost but cheapest per-inference at volume.
Red flags — don't fine-tune when…
You have < 50 examples Task keeps changing No eval benchmark exists Data quality is unknown Prompt hasn't been tried No labeled data budget

LoRA Low-Rank Adaptation
A parameter-efficient technique. Instead of updating all weights, LoRA injects small trainable rank-decomposition matrices into each layer. Only ~0.1–1% of parameters are trained.

Key param: rank r (4–64) Higher r = more capacity Lower r = faster, cheaper
LoRA + SFT or DPO: LoRA is a training efficiency technique, not a loss function. You combine LoRA with SFT or DPO as your objective.

Quality > quantity

100 perfectly curated examples often beats 10,000 noisy ones. Each bad example teaches the model a wrong pattern.

Rule of thumb: spend 50% of your budget on data curation, 30% on training, 20% on evaluation.

Fine-tuning proposal: Legal document summarizer

Problem
A mid-size law firm processes 200+ contracts per week. Associates spend 2–3 hours summarizing each. Summaries vary in format and consistently miss key clauses like indemnity, termination, and governing law. GPT-4 with prompting produces inconsistent structure.
Why fine-tune (not just prompt)
Prompting was tried first. The model ignores formatting instructions ~20% of the time and misses domain-specific clause detection. The output format must be strictly consistent for downstream legal review software to parse it. This is a classic fine-tuning trigger: consistent structured output + domain vocabulary + scale.
Chosen approach
SFT + LoRA on a 7B open-source model (e.g. Mistral 7B). SFT because we have gold outputs (existing partner-reviewed summaries). LoRA because we can run this on a single A100 GPU, keeping cost under $500 for training. Not DPO — we don't need preference learning, we need format and clause extraction.
Data plan
2,000 contracts → partner-reviewed summaries (already in firm archives). 1,600 train / 200 validation / 200 test. Data cleaning: redact client names, normalize formatting, verify all 8 target clauses are labeled in every summary. Budget: ~$15k in legal staff time for curation.
Evaluation
Primary metric: clause recall (did the model find all 8 clause types?). Secondary: ROUGE-L vs partner summaries. Human eval: 50 contracts reviewed by senior associate. Target: >92% clause recall, >80% associate approval.
Cost estimate
Training: ~$200 GPU cost on Lambda Labs. Data prep: $15k staff time. Infrastructure: $500/month self-hosted inference. Break-even: at 200 contracts/week × 2.5hr saved per contract × $100/hr associate time = $50k/month saved. Payback in under 1 month.
Risks & mitigations
Risk 1 — model hallucinates clause content. Mitigation: output always cites exact contract page + line. Risk 2 — data drift (new contract types). Mitigation: monthly retraining cycle with new examples flagged by users. Risk 3 — confidentiality. Mitigation: self-hosted model, no third-party API.
